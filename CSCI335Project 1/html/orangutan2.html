<!--[H2]--><span class="marker h2"></span><section id="ref338572" data-level="1"><h2 class="h1">Natural history</h2>
<p class="topic-paragraph">Orangutans are not as powerfully built as the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/Gorilla-primate-genus" class="md-crosslink">gorilla</a> but are larger than the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/chimpanzee" class="md-crosslink">chimpanzee</a>. The adult male is typically twice the size of the female and may attain a height of 1.3 metres (4.3 feet) and a weight of 130 kg (285 pounds) in the wild; females weigh 37 kg (82 pounds) or less. Older males develop wide cheek pads, a unique feature among <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/primate-mammal" class="md-crosslink">primates</a>. The typically dark tan or brownish <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/human-skin" class="md-crosslink">skin</a> is covered with relatively coarse and usually sparse red <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/hair-anatomy" class="md-crosslink">hair</a>. Adult males and some older adult females may have partially or entirely bare backs, but the hair on a male can be so long as to look like a cape when he moves his arms.</p><!--[P2]--><span class="marker p2"></span><div class="assemblies"><div class=""><figure class="md-assembly card print-false" data-assembly-id="6777"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><a style="--aspect-ratio: 16/9" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/51/3251-050-2433CE67/orangutan-cheek-pads.jpg" class="position-relative d-flex align-items-center justify-content-center media-overlay-link card-media" data-href="/media/1/430975/6777"><img loading="lazy" src="https://cdn.britannica.com/s:690x388,c:crop/51/3251-050-2433CE67/orangutan-cheek-pads.jpg" alt="Male orangutan (Pongo pygmaeus) with cheek pads." data-width="950" data-height="1206"><button class="btn btn-circle position-absolute shadow btn-white"><em class="material-icons link-blue" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></button></a></div><figcaption class="card-body"><div class="md-assembly-caption text-muted font-14 font-serif">Male orangutan (<em>Pongo pygmaeus</em>) with cheek pads.</div><cite class="credit d-block mt-5">Russ Kinne/Photo Researchers</cite></figcaption></figure></div></div><!--[AM2]--><span class="marker AM2 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD2]--><span class="marker MOD2 mod-inline"></span>
<section id="ref338573" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Diet and locomotion</h2>
<p class="topic-paragraph">Orangutans are the largest arboreal animals, spending more than 90 percent of their waking hours in the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/plant/tree" class="md-crosslink">trees</a>. During the day most of their time is divided equally between resting and feeding. Orangutans are predominantly ripe-fruit eaters, although they consume more than 400 different types of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/food" class="md-crosslink">food</a>, including <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/invertebrate" class="md-crosslink">invertebrates</a> and, on rare and opportunistic occasions, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/meat" class="md-crosslink">meat</a>. Almost every night orangutans construct a sleeping platform in the trees by bending and breaking branches, leaves, and twigs. Unlike the African <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/ape" class="md-crosslink">apes</a>, orangutans frequently use vegetation to protect themselves from the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/rain" class="md-crosslink">rain</a>.</p><!--[P3]--><span class="marker p3"></span><div class="assemblies"><div class=""><figure class="md-assembly card print-false" data-assembly-id="158299"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><a style="--aspect-ratio: 16/9" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/91/146391-050-1DB7B52A/Orangutan-tree-branches.jpg" class="position-relative d-flex align-items-center justify-content-center media-overlay-link card-media" data-href="/media/1/430975/158299"><img loading="lazy" src="https://cdn.britannica.com/s:690x388,c:crop/91/146391-050-1DB7B52A/Orangutan-tree-branches.jpg" alt="Orangutan (Pongo pygmaeus) swinging along tree branches." data-width="533" data-height="800"><button class="btn btn-circle position-absolute shadow btn-white"><em class="material-icons link-blue" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></button></a></div><figcaption class="card-body"><div class="md-assembly-caption text-muted font-14 font-serif">Orangutan (<em>Pongo pygmaeus</em>) swinging along tree branches.</div><cite class="credit d-block mt-5">© UryadnikovS/Fotolia</cite></figcaption></figure></div></div><!--[AM3]--><span class="marker AM3 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD3]--><span class="marker MOD3 mod-inline"></span>
<p class="topic-paragraph">In addition to feeding and resting, orangutans also spend short periods of time traveling through the forest canopy, where they typically scramble by using all four hands and feet. Orangutans occasionally swing through the trees using only their arms (<a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/brachiation" class="md-crosslink">brachiation</a>). Although their <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/leg-anatomy" class="md-crosslink">legs</a> are short, their arms are proportionately the longest of those of the great apes. The hooklike hands have long <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/finger-measurement" class="md-crosslink">fingers</a> and palms with short <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/thumb" class="md-crosslink">thumbs</a>. The feet resemble the hands in having opposable big toes that are similar to the thumbs. Another arboreal <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/adaptation" class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off" data-term="adaptation">adaptation</a> is flexible hip joints that allow orangutans similar movement in their legs and arms. On the ground orangutans are slow; a person can easily keep pace with them. They are not <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/knuckle-anatomy" class="md-crosslink">knuckle</a> walkers like the African apes but instead walk on closed fists or extended palms.</p><!--[P4]--><span class="marker p4"></span><div class="assemblies medialist slider js-slider position-relative d-inline-flex align-items-center mw-100"><div class="slider-container <section id="ref338574" data-level="2"><h2 class="h2">Reproduction</h2>
<p class="topic-paragraph">There are two phases of sexual maturation among males—adult and subadult. Adult males are larger and exhibit striking secondary sexual characteristics, particularly the flat and prominent <span id="ref828226"></span>cheek pads that develop along the sides of the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/face-anatomy" class="md-crosslink">face</a>. The pads <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/enhance" class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off" data-term="enhance">enhance</a> the size of the head and are linked with increased levels of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/testosterone" class="md-crosslink">testosterone</a>. Adult males also have a throat pouch that serves as a <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/resonating" class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off" data-term="resonating">resonating</a> chamber for the “<span id="ref828225"></span>long call,” a sequence of roars that can sometimes be heard for 2 km (1.2 miles). Males typically <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/vocalization" class="md-crosslink">vocalize</a> for a minute or more; <span id="ref875302"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/mating-call" class="md-crosslink">calls</a> up to five minutes in length have been recorded, giving the call its name. Females virtually never give the full sequence of the long call, as it serves to space males and attract sexually receptive females. Otherwise, orangutans are generally silent. Subadult males lack the wide cheek pads and large throat pouch, and they generally do not long call. Although smaller than adult males, subadults are still as large as or larger than adult females. Subadults may remain in this state for 10 to 20 years. This arrested development has been linked with stress associated with the presence of adult males.</p><!--[P5]--><span class="marker p5"></span><!--[AM5]--><span class="marker AM5 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD5]--><span class="marker MOD5 mod-inline"></span>
<p class="topic-paragraph">Orangutans live in a semisolitary social organization that is unique among <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/monkey" class="md-crosslink">monkeys</a> and apes. <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/population-biology-and-anthropology" class="md-crosslink">Population</a> densities usually average only two to three individuals per square kilometre (about five to seven per square mile), with adult males having larger home ranges than females. Adult males are the most solitary, avoiding each other and associating only with consorting females or former consorts. Subadult males associate primarily with females. Adult females live with their dependent young, but adolescent females are almost <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/gregarious" class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off" data-term="gregarious">gregarious</a>. Sexually receptive females may attract several males, both adult and subadult. Males, adults in particular, behave aggressively toward other males at this time, with combat taking place in the presence of receptive females. Most <span id="ref828227"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/mating" class="md-crosslink">mating</a> takes place in the <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context" class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off" data-term="context">context</a> of consortships that last 3 to 10 days and are correlated with <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/ovulation" class="md-crosslink">ovulation</a>. Subadult males often forcibly copulate with females at times other than during ovulation.</p><!--[P6]--><span class="marker p6"></span><div class="assemblies"><div class=""><figure class="md-assembly card print-false" data-assembly-id="56346"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><a style="--aspect-ratio: 16/9" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/69/65969-050-8E4B0AB9/Orangutan.jpg" class="position-relative d-flex align-items-center justify-content-center media-overlay-link card-media" data-href="/media/1/430975/56346"><img loading="lazy" src="https://cdn.britannica.com/s:690x388,c:crop/69/65969-050-8E4B0AB9/Orangutan.jpg" alt="Orangutan (Pongo pygmaeus)." data-width="1600" data-height="1530"><button class="btn btn-circle position-absolute shadow btn-white"><em class="material-icons link-blue" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></button></a></div><figcaption class="card-body"><div class="md-assembly-caption text-muted font-14 font-serif">Orangutan (<em>Pongo pygmaeus</em>).</div><cite class="credit d-block mt-5">© guenterguni/iStock.com</cite></figcaption></figure></div></div><!--[AM6]--><span class="marker AM6 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD6]--><span class="marker MOD6 mod-inline"></span>
<p class="topic-paragraph">Female orangutans have the longest breeding interval of any <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/mammal" class="md-crosslink">mammal</a>, giving birth on average once every eight years. Wild females generally first give birth when they are 15 or 16 years of age, but females as young as 7 have given birth in captivity. <span id="ref875303"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/gestation" class="md-crosslink">Gestation</a> is about eight months. Newborns weigh less than 1.5 kg (3.3 pounds) and have prominent white patches around their eyes and mouths as well as scattered over their bodies. Slow growth and development are consistent with the orangutan’s long life span—60 years has been documented in captivity.</p><!--[P7]--><span class="marker p7"></span><div class="assemblies"><div class=""><figure class="md-assembly card print-false" data-assembly-id="158297"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><a style="--aspect-ratio: 16/9" href="https://cdn.britannica.com/38/146438-050-F90A421C/orangutan-baby.jpg" class="position-relative d-flex align-items-center justify-content-center media-overlay-link card-media" data-href="/media/1/430975/158297"><img loading="lazy" src="https://cdn.britannica.com/s:690x388,c:crop/38/146438-050-F90A421C/orangutan-baby.jpg" alt="Adult orangutan (Pongo pygmaeus) with baby." data-width="800" data-height="532"><button class="btn btn-circle position-absolute shadow btn-white"><em class="material-icons link-blue" data-icon="zoom_in"></em></button></a></div><figcaption class="card-body"><div class="md-assembly-caption text-muted font-14 font-serif">Adult orangutan (<em>Pongo pygmaeus</em>) with baby.</div><cite class="credit d-block mt-5">© Kitch Bain/Fotolia</cite></figcaption></figure></div></div><!--[AM7]--><span class="marker AM7 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD7]--><span class="marker MOD7 mod-inline"></span></section>
