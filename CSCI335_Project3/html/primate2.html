<p align="left"><b><strong><font face="Times New Roman"><font size="1"><br>
</font></font></strong> </b><font face="Arial">The
<span lang="en-us"><font color="#333333"><b>o</b></font></span><font color="#333333"><b>rder
<i>Primates</i></b></font> <a href="sounds/Primates.mp3">
<img alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" src="images/horn.gif" width="20" height="19"></a>
includes humans, apes, monkeys<span lang="en-us">, and</span> prosimians.&nbsp; Many of them may be familiar, but it would not be surprising if you cannot
immediately visualize prosimians (like the ring-tailed lemurs in the picture on the
right)</font><span lang="en-us"><font face="Arial">.</font></span></p>
<p align="left">
<font face="Arial"><span lang="en-us">How many living primate</span>
<a href="glossary.htm#species">species</a><span lang="en-us"> exist today is not
clear.&nbsp; The number </span>varies depending on whether closely related
groups are <span lang="en-us">considered to be</span> varieties<span lang="en-us">
of each other</span> or distinct species.&nbsp; Some <span lang="en-us">
<a href="glossary.htm#taxonomy">taxonomic</a> </span>splitters classify up
to 350 species<span lang="en-us">,
while lumpers include as few as 190.&nbsp; Most estimates are in the range
of 230-270.&nbsp; This ambiguity may be partly resolved in the future</span> <span lang="en-us">with </span>DNA sequencing data.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;
Compounding the problem is the fact that e</span>very few years new
<span lang="en-us">species of primates</span> are found.&nbsp;
The tropical forests of South America, Africa, and Southeast Asia may still be hiding<span lang="en-us">
ones that are unknown to the scientific world</span>.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;
However, it is likely that all major groupings of primates have been
discovered.</span></font></p>
<p align="left"><font face="Arial">M<span lang="en-us">any</span> primate
species are now <span lang="en-us">in </span>danger<span lang="en-us"> of
becoming extinct</span>.&nbsp; The primary cause is deforestation, driven ultimately by human
population growth.&nbsp; Additional pressure is placed on
primate populations by humans hunting them <span lang="en-us">to sell </span>for food and pets.&nbsp;
Monkeys and apes are popular sources of &quot;bush meat&quot; in West Africa.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;
At least 22 species of primates and many other kinds of wild animals are being hunted
in Africa for this purpose.&nbsp; Despite the fact that the sale of &quot;bush
meat&quot; is outlawed in most countries, it is now being sold illegally in
Europe and North America.&nbsp; In recent years, it could be bought at stores that cater to
African immigrants in Paris, New York, Chicago, San Francisco, Montreal, and
some other major cities.&nbsp; </span></font></p>
<p align="left"><font face="Arial">Today, non-human
primates are limited in their natural habitats primarily to the tropical and subtropical
<span lang="en-us">regions</span> of the <a href="glossary.htm#Old_World">New and Old Worlds</a>.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;
They have never lived in Australia and most of the islands in the Pacific
ocean.&nbsp; However, the earliest primates also lived in North America and
Europe beginning around 55-50 million years ago.</span></font></p>

of Primates</span></font></b></font></p>

<p align="left"><font face="Arial">Primates are
surprisingly variable in size.&nbsp; They range all the way from the
<span lang="en-us">pygmy </span>mouse lemur that
weighs <span lang="en-us">less than 2</span> ounces<span lang="en-us"> (55 g.) and can fit in the palm of your
hand</span> to the gorilla that grows to as much as 4<span lang="en-us">4</span>0 pounds<span lang="en-us">
(200 kg.)</span> in the wild
and even heavier in captivity.&nbsp; <span lang="en-us">Still</span> larger primates existed in the past but are now
extinct.</font></p>
<p align="left"><font face="Arial">Primates are
generally lively, clever, and very successful at adapting to different environmental
opportunities.&nbsp; Physically, however, they are relatively unspecialized compared to
animals such as birds, horses, and cats.&nbsp; Primates are not particularly
fast runners, they do not have the sharpest hearing, they cannot fly, nor
are they efficient hunters.</font></p>

<p align="left"><font face="Arial">With the exception
of spider monkeys, all of the primates have retained the ancient <a href="glossary.htm#mammal">mammalian</a> trait of
<font color="#333333"><b>pentadactylism</b></font> <a href="sounds/pentadactylism.mp3">
<img alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" src="images/horn.gif" width="20" height="19"></a>, which is having five fingers on each hand and five
toes on each foot.&nbsp; An increasing refinement of the hands and
feet for grasping objects has been a hallmark of primate evolution.&nbsp; Their fingers
and toes are mobile and have very sensitive tactile pads at the tips, unlike most
other mammals.&nbsp; At least some digits on all primates also have flat nails in
place of rigid claws.&nbsp; This makes the manipulation of bits of food and other objects
much easier.</font></p>
<p align="left"><font face="Arial">The grasping, or <font color="#333333"><b>prehensile</b></font>
<a href="sounds/prehensile.mp3">
<img alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" src="images/horn.gif" width="20" height="19"></a>, ability of
primate hands varies significantly also with
the degree of opposability of the thumb. &nbsp;Partially rotating the thumb and pressing
it forcefully towards the fingers provides a secure grip for hanging on branches and for
manipulating small objects. &nbsp;All primates, with the exception of humans, have
prehensile feet in addition to hands. &nbsp;However, few have the high degree of thumb
opposability<span lang="en-us"> and strength</span> typical of humans.</font></p>
<p align="left"><font face="Arial">The
small New World monkeys called marmosets and tamarins usually hang onto a tree
branch by pressing against it with their palms and using all of their bent fingers
together as counter pressure in a hook-like <b>power grip</b>.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;
</span>The more dexterous
monkeys and apes can also use a secure power grip formed by the
partial flexion of the fingers and the palm with counter force applied by the thumb.<span lang="en-us">
</span>Humans and
some apes also can employ <font color="#333333"><b>precision grips</b></font> formed by pinching with the tips of their
flexed forefingers and the thumb.&nbsp; This allows their hands to be used effectively for
manipulating <span lang="en-us">small</span> objects<span lang="en-us">, such
as the fork in the hand of the chimpanzee shown below</span>.</font></p>

<p align="left"><font face="Arial">All primates
have a marked tendency towards erectness in their upper bodies.&nbsp; This can be seen in
their sitting and standing postures as well as occasional <a href="glossary.htm#bipedal">bipedalism</a> <a href="sounds/bipedalism.mp3">
<img alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" src="images/horn.gif" width="20" height="19"></a>.&nbsp;
They also have shoulder joints that are unusually flexible for mammals.&nbsp; This is due
to <span lang="en-us">secure </span>ball joints and strong
<font color="#333333"><b>clavicles</b></font> <a href="sounds/clavicles.mp3">
<img alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" src="images/horn.gif" width="20" height="19"></a><span lang="en-us">,</span> or
collarbones, which have allowed them to use their arms very effectively
in climbing trees.&nbsp; Clavicles are another ancient
mammalian trait that primates have retained.</font></p>
<p align="left"><font face="Arial">In primate
evolution, there was a progressive reduction in nose size and in the <a href="glossary.htm#olfactory">olfactory</a> <a href="sounds/olfactory.mp3">
<img alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" src="images/horn.gif" width="20" height="19"></a> areas of
the brain.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; </span>Lemurs are <span lang="en-us">an
exception.&nbsp; They are </span>very much like the early primates<span lang="en-us">
50 million years ago</span>.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; </span>Note
the marked difference between lemur, monkey, and human noses in the photos
below.&nbsp; Most
monkeys and apes have <span lang="en-us">evolved </span>relatively small noses like us,
while lemurs have<span lang="en-us"> retained</span> long snouts similar to foxes and <span lang="en-us">
raccoons</span>.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;
It is not surprising that lemurs have a comparatively good sense of smell.</span></font></p>

<p align="left"><font face="Arial">Most mammals have about the same number of genes that
code for odor receptors in their nasal tracks.&nbsp; However, the majority
of these genes in humans, and presumably other large primates, are no longer
functional.&nbsp; Recent research has shown that of the approximately 1,000
human olfactory receptor genes, only 347 remain functional.&nbsp; The rest
have mutations that deactivate them.&nbsp; Does this<span lang="en-us">
really</span> mean that people have a
poor sense of smell?&nbsp; The answer is both yes and no<span lang="en-us">.</span>&nbsp; We are poor
at sensing short-chain chemical compounds but good at discriminating between
complex long-chain ones such as those of many foods and flowers.&nbsp; Dogs
are just the opposite in this.&nbsp; However, the smelling ability of dogs
is far superior to people in one way.&nbsp; Fewer molecules of a substance
are usually needed for dogs to detect it.</font></p>
<p align="left"><font face="Arial">As the
primate nose progressively shrank in size over millions of
years, there was a corresponding increase in
visual capabilities.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; As much as 50% of the
<a href="glossary.htm#cerebral_cortex">cerebral cortex</a> in some species
of monkeys is involved in visual processing.&nbsp; </span>Many primates have color vision comparable to our own.&nbsp;
All have <a href="glossary.htm#binocular_vision">binocular vision</a> with fields
of view that significantly overlap, resulting in true
<b> <font color="#333333">
three dimensional
(3-D) depth
perception</font></b> or <b>stereoscopic vision</b>.&nbsp; At the same time,<span lang="en-us">
the field of view for</span> <a href="glossary.htm#peripheral_vision">peripheral
vision</a> was reduced.</font></p>

<p align="left"><span lang="en-us"><font face="Arial">Depth perception is an
invaluable tool for animals that need to move quickly.&nbsp; It allows them to judge
the distances to important objects in their environment.&nbsp; For monkeys
jumping from branch to branch high up in the canopies of forests, this is a
matter of life and death.&nbsp; Likewise, accurate depth perception is a
critical capability for hunters, such as cats and hawks, who need to
accurately judge distances in the pursuit
of their prey.&nbsp; In contrast, it is often preferable to have greater
peripheral vision if you are the prey.&nbsp; Gazelles, rabbits, and chickens
have nearly a 360 field of view, but relatively little of that is
stereoscopic vision.</font></span></p>
<p align="left"><font face="Arial">Compared to
most other animals, primate brains are large relative to their body size.&nbsp; Those
areas of the brain that are involved with controlling <a href="glossary.htm#manual_dexterity">manual dexterity</a>, eye-hand coordination, and
stereoscopic vision have particularly
expanded.&nbsp; These traits were probably selected by nature mostly due to their
usefulness in traveling in trees<span lang="en-us">, manipulating objects,</span>
and getting food.</font></p>

<p align="left"><font face="Arial"><span lang="en-us">The more capable uterus
and placenta of primates</span> <span lang="en-us">support</span> long <font color="#333333"><b>gestation</b></font> periods<span lang="en-us">,
or pregnancies,</span> for
animals of their size.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; For example, the mouse lemur
has a gestation period of 60 days, but it is only 20 days for a comparable
size rodent mouse.&nbsp; </span></font><span lang="en-us">
<font face="Arial">M</font></span><font face="Arial"><span lang="en-us">acaques,
which are one of the most common groups of monkey species in the Old World,
have 13.5-15.5 week gestations.&nbsp; Among comparable size domesticated
dogs, it is only about 9 weeks.&nbsp; </span>The result<span lang="en-us"> of long
gestations</span> is that offspring are born more mature and,
therefore, have a greater chance of survival.&nbsp; Primate mothers also are generally
very protective and nurturing with their young.&nbsp; Subsequently, primates do not need
to have many offspring to maintain their population numbers.&nbsp; Multiple births at any
one time are rare<span lang="en-us"> for them</span>.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;
</span></font><span lang="en-us"><font face="Arial">The time gap between
generations is surprisingly long for the larger primates.&nbsp; Among humans
today, parents are on average 29 years older than their children.&nbsp; By
comparison, chimpanzee mothers are on average 25 years older than their
offspring.&nbsp; Among female gorillas, it is 19.3 years.</font></span></p>
<p align="left"><font face="Arial"><span lang="en-us">There frequently
is a correlation between body size and longevity in the animal kingdom.&nbsp;
Species with larger bodies generally live longer.&nbsp; However, p</span>rimates<span lang="en-us">
</span>have unusually long natural life<span lang="en-us"> </span>spans<span lang="en-us"> for
their size</span>.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; For instance, mouse
lemurs live about 8 years.&nbsp; Rodent mice
rarely reach 2 years.&nbsp; Rats, which are slightly larger than mouse
lemurs, can live 3 years.&nbsp; Big monkeys, such as baboons, live up to 30
years.&nbsp; Male baboons can weigh as much as 90 pounds.&nbsp;
Comparable size domesticated dogs have a life expectancy of only 10-12
years.&nbsp; </span>With
good medical care, chimpanzees can live nearly as long as people<span lang="en-us">
(50-75+ years)</span>.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; This is not surprising
given that they are anatomically very similar to us.&nbsp; Research done
by Herman Pontzer of Hunter College suggests that the unusual longevity of
primates is due to the fact that they expend only 50% as many calories a day
compared to other animals their size.&nbsp; Slower metabolism rates are
associated with slower rates of growth, reproduction, and aging in mammals.</span></font></p>
<p align="left"><font face="Arial">Most
primates have adapted to an <font color="#333333"><b>arboreal</b></font>, or tree living, way of life.&nbsp; Even the <a href="glossary.htm#terrestrial">terrestrial</a>
<a href="sounds/terrestrial.mp3">
<img alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" src="images/horn.gif" width="20" height="19"></a>
ones usually sleep in the
trees.&nbsp; The most notable exceptions are humans
and gorillas.&nbsp; Both of these species live on the
ground with occasional tree climbing for fun, especially
by children.&nbsp; Almost all primates are <font color="#333333"><b>diurnal</b></font>, which is to say, they are active
during the day and sleep at night.</font></p>
<p align="left"><font face="Arial">Generally,
primates are highly social animals.&nbsp; Most of their waking hours are spent socializing
with each other.&nbsp; This is made easier by complex <a href="glossary.htm#vocalizations">vocalizations</a> and<span lang="en-us">
visual</span> <a href="glossary.htm#displays">displays</a>.&nbsp; In addition, they regularly groom
each other, thereby keeping clean and satisfying psychological needs<span lang="en-us">
at the same time</span>.&nbsp;
<font color="#333333"><b>Grooming</b></font> is
a very pleasurable activity for primates, including humans.&nbsp; It is important to note that the few <a href="glossary.htm#nocturnal">nocturnal</a>
<a href="sounds/nocturnal.mp3">
<img alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" src="images/horn.gif" width="20" height="19"></a>

<p align="left"><font face="Arial">Primates
have been very successful animals due largely to the fact that they are intelligent and
opportunistic in getting food.&nbsp; Most are unusually adaptable in diet.&nbsp; This has
tremendous evolutionarily selective value.&nbsp; Many species are <a href="glossary.htm#omnivorous">omnivorous</a>
<a href="sounds/omnivorous.mp3">
<img alt="click this icon to hear the preceding term pronounced" align="absbottom" border="0" src="images/horn.gif" width="20" height="19"></a>, though vegetable foods usually make up the
bulk of calories consumed by most primate species because they are easier to obtain.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp;
</span>By comparison, animals such as koalas and giant pandas are generally less
successful because they are extremely limited in the kinds of foods that they
can or will eat.&nbsp; Koalas subsist on the leaves of a few species of
eucalyptus, and giant pandas primarily eat the shoots of a small number of
bamboo species.&nbsp; If these food sources are not available, koalas and
giant pandas die.&nbsp; Not surprisingly, their highly limited range of foods
restricts where they can live.&nbsp; This is not the case with many primate
species.</font></p>
<p align="center"><font face="Times New Roman" color="#008080" size="1"><br>
</font><font face="Arial" color="#808080" size="5"><b>Are Primates Good</b></font><b><font face="Arial" color="#808080" size="5">
Pets?</font></b></p>
<p align="left"><font face="Arial">Often people who have not had the responsibility of
taking care of a monkey or ape in captivity think that they would be great
pets.&nbsp; After all, they are cute when they are youngsters and remarkably
human-like.&nbsp; However, they generally make very poor pets.&nbsp; As one
monkey owner said &quot;People find out when it's too late.&nbsp; After six
months, they're like a two year-old that can fly.&quot;&nbsp; Because they are
intelligent, very active, sometimes unpredictable, and easily bored, they
frequently get into trouble when left alone.&nbsp; In addition to being
destructive, most are as messy as human babies.&nbsp; Unlike humans,
however, they remain this way all of their unusually long lives.&nbsp;
Owning large apes in particular is a lifelong commitment that takes
considerable patience, energy, and money.<span lang="en-us">&nbsp; Male
chimpanzees can be surprisingly violent in their interactions with
each other and humans.&nbsp; In recent years, several people have been
extensively maimed and even killed by their pet chimpanzees in the U.S.</span></font></p>
