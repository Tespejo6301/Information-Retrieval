<p>Meiosis describes the process of cell division by which <strong>gametes</strong> are made. In this process, we begin with a cell with double the normal amount of DNA, and end up with 4 <strong>non-identical haploid daughter gametes</strong> after two divisions.</p>
<p>There are six stages within each of the divisions, namely prophase, prometaphase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase and cytokinesis. In this article, we will look at the stages of meiosis and consider its significance in disease.</p>

</div>
</div></div></div></div><div class="article-row"><div class="container"><div class="row"><div class="col-12 col-lg-8 pr-lg-5 article-col col-p-12"><h2><span id="Meiosis_I">Meiosis I</span></h2>
<p>In meiosis I, <strong>homologous chromosomes</strong> are separated into two cells such that there is one chromosome (consisting of two chromatids) per chromosome pair in each daughter cell, i.e. two chromosomes total.</p>
<h3><span id="Prophase_I">Prophase I</span></h3>
<p>Prior to prophase, chromosomes replicate to form <strong>sister chromatids</strong>. There are initially four chromatids (c) and two chromosomes (n) for each of the 23 chromosome pairs (4c, 2n). The nuclear envelope disintegrates and the chromosomes begin to condense. <strong>Spindle fibres</strong> appear which are important for successful division of the chromosomes.</p>
<p>To further increase <strong>genetic diversity</strong>, homologous chromosomes exchange small parts of themselves, such that one chromosome contains both maternal and paternal DNA. This process is known as <strong>crossing over</strong>, and the points at which this occurs on a chromosome are referred to as <strong>chiasmata.</strong></p>
<h3><span id="Prometaphase_I">Prometaphase I</span></h3>
<p>Spindle fibres attach to the chromosomes at a points along the chromosomes called <strong>centromeres</strong>. While this is happening, the chromosomes continue to condense.</p>
<div id="attachment_13754" class="wp-caption aligncenter">
<div class="copyright">Adapted from work by Ali Zifan [CC BY-SA 4.0 (https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0)], via Wikimedia Commons</div>
<a href="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/ProphaseI.png" title=" Figure 2 – Image of prometaphase I." rel="lightbox"><img loading="lazy" class="size-medium wp-image-13754" src="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/ProphaseI-300x270.png" alt="" width="300" height="270" srcset="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/ProphaseI-300x270.png 300w, https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/ProphaseI.png 416w" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px"></a><p class="wp-caption-text"> Figure 2 – Image of prometaphase I.</p>
</div>
<h3><span id="Metaphase_I">Metaphase I</span></h3>
<p>Maternal and paternal versions of the same chromosome <strong>(homologous chromosomes)</strong> align along the equator of the cell.<strong> </strong>A process called <strong>independent</strong><strong> assortment</strong> occurs – this is when maternal and paternal chromosomes line up randomly align themselves on either side of the equator. This is turn determines to which gamete chromosomes are allocated to, which leads to genetic diversity among offspring.</p>
<div id="attachment_13755" class="wp-caption aligncenter">
<div class="copyright">Adapted from work by Ali Zifan [CC BY-SA 4.0 (https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0)], via Wikimedia Commons</div>

</div>
<h3><span id="Anaphase_I">Anaphase I</span></h3>
<p>Here, each of the homologous chromosomes get pulled towards opposite poles of the cell as the spindle fibres retract. This equally divides the DNA between the two cells which will be formed.</p>
<div id="attachment_13756" class="wp-caption aligncenter">
<div class="copyright">Adapted from work by Ali Zifan [CC BY-SA 4.0 (https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0)], via Wikimedia Commons</div>
<a href="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Anaphase-I.png" title=" Figure 4 – Image of Anaphase I." rel="lightbox"><img loading="lazy" class="size-medium wp-image-13756" src="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Anaphase-I-271x300.png" alt="" width="271" height="300" srcset="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Anaphase-I-271x300.png 271w, https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Anaphase-I.png 369w" sizes="(max-width: 271px) 100vw, 271px"></a><p class="wp-caption-text"> Figure 4 – Image of Anaphase I.</p>
</div>
<h3><span id="Telophase_I_and_Cytokinesis_I">Telophase I and Cytokinesis I</span></h3>
<p>During telophase I, the nuclear envelope reforms and spindle fibres disappear. In cytokinesis I, the cytoplasm and cell divides resulting in two cells that are technically haploid – there is one chromosome and two chromatids for each chromosome (2c, n).</p>
<div id="attachment_13758" class="wp-caption aligncenter">

</div></div></div></div><div class="article-row"><div class="container"><div class="row"><div class="col-12 col-lg-8 pr-lg-5 article-col col-p-12"><h2><span id="Meiosis_II">Meiosis II</span></h2>
<h3><span id="Prophase_II_and_Prometaphase_II">Prophase II and Prometaphase II</span></h3>
<p>These stages are identical to their counterparts in meiosis I.</p>
<h3><span id="Metaphase_II">Metaphase II</span></h3>
<p>In metaphase II, chromosomes line up in single file along the equator of the cell. This is in contrast to metaphase I, where chromosomes line up in homologous pairs.</p>
<div id="attachment_13759" class="wp-caption aligncenter">
<div class="copyright">Adapted from work by Ali Zifan [CC BY-SA 4.0 (https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0)], via Wikimedia Commons</div>

</div>
<h3><span id="Anaphase_II">Anaphase II</span></h3>
<p>Next, <strong>sister chromatids</strong> are pulled to opposite poles of the equator.</p>
<div id="attachment_13760" class="wp-caption aligncenter">
<div class="copyright">Adapted from work by Ali Zifan [CC BY-SA 4.0 (https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0)], via Wikimedia Commons</div>

</div>
<h3><span id="Telophase_II">Telophase II</span></h3>
<p>This stage is the same as telophase I.</p>
<h3><span id="Cytokinesis_II">Cytokinesis II</span></h3>
<p>Again, the cytoplasm and cell divides producing 2 non-identical haploid daughter cells. As this is happening in both cells produced by meiosis I, the net product is <strong>4 non-identical haploid daughter cells</strong>, each containing one chromosome consisting of one chromatid (1c, 1n). These are fully formed gametes.</p>
<div id="attachment_13762" class="wp-caption aligncenter">

</div>

<h2><span id="Clinical_Relevance_8211_Chromosomal_abnormalities">Clinical Relevance – Chromosomal abnormalities</span></h2>
<p>Chromosome abnormalities occur in approximately 0.6% of live births, however are common in pregnancy losses. Errors in the process of meiosis can lead to abnormalities in either chromosome number and structure.</p>
<p><strong>Abnormalities in chromosome number</strong> include <strong>aneuploidy</strong>, where there is loss or gain of a whole chromosome. This is often due to <strong>nondisjunction</strong> where there is failed separation of chromosomes during anaphase, so either whole chromosomes (error occurring in meiosis I) or chromatids (error occurring in meiosis II) move to the same pole of the cell. This leaves one gamete short of some genetic information, and the other with additional genetic information.</p>
<ul>
<li>Monosomy – one copy of a chromosome, e.g. Turner syndrome (45, X)</li>
<li>Trisomy – three copies of a chromosome, e.g. Edward’s syndrome (trisomy 18)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Abnormalities in chromosome structure </strong>are often due to translocations, where there is exchange of material between two chromosomes, resulting in an abnormal rearrangement. If there is no gain or loss of genetic material, this is a balanced translocation, however if the exchange of chromosomal material results in extra or missing genes in a daughter cell, it is known as unbalanced and can have clinical effects.</p>
<p>There are two types of chromosomal translocation. <strong>Reciprocal translocations</strong> take place when the chromosomes break within the arms of the chromosome and Robertsonian<strong> translocations</strong> take place when whole chromosomes join end to end.</p>
<div id="attachment_19311" class="wp-caption aligncenter">
<div class="copyright"> By <a href="http://teachmeseries.com" style="color: #fff !important" target="_blank">TeachMeSeries Ltd</a> (2021)</div>
<a href="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/translocation.jpg" title=" Figure 9 – Reciprocal and Robertsonian translocation" rel="lightbox"><img loading="lazy" class="size-full wp-image-19311" src="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/translocation.jpg" alt="" width="1024" height="408" srcset="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/translocation.jpg 1024w, https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/translocation-300x120.jpg 300w, https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/translocation-768x306.jpg 768w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px"></a><p class="wp-caption-text"> Figure 9 – Reciprocal and Robertsonian translocation</p>
</div>
<p><strong>Anaphase lag</strong> can occur where chromosomes are left behind due to defects in the spindle fibres or attachment to chromosomes. This differs from non-disjunction as neither cell receives the chromosome/chromatid, leaving both daughter cells short of genetic information</p>
<p></p>
</div>

<h2><span id="Clinical_Relevance_8211_Down8217s_syndrome">Clinical Relevance – Down’s syndrome</span></h2>
<p>Down’s syndrome occurs when there is an extra copy of chromosome 21 (trisomy 21). Typical signs and symptoms include muscular hypotonia, a single palmar crease, protruding tongue, congenital heart malformations and distinguishable facies.</p>
<p>95% of cases occur when there are three separate copies of chromosome 21 due to nondisjunction. 4% of cases are secondary to Robertsonian translocation, where a chromosome acquires a large chunk of chromosome 21, effectively leading to three copies. The remaining 1% of cases are due to <strong>post-zygotic mosaicism</strong>, where non-disjunction occurs in early embryonic development, meaning some cells have 46 chromosomes as usual, while others have trisomy 21. This generally leads to milder symptoms due to the presence of normal cells.</p>

<div class="col-12 col-lg-8 content">
<p>Meiosis describes the process of cell division by which <strong>gametes</strong> are made. In this process, we begin with a cell with double the normal amount of DNA, and end up with 4 <strong>non-identical haploid daughter gametes</strong> after two divisions.</p>
<p>There are six stages within each of the divisions, namely prophase, prometaphase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase and cytokinesis. In this article, we will look at the stages of meiosis and consider its significance in disease.</p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="caption">&#91;caption id="attachment_13751" align="aligncenter" width="768"&#93;<img class="wp-image-13751 size-full" src="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Meiosis_Overview_new.png" alt="" width="768" height="483" /> Figure 1 - An overview of meiosis&#91;/caption&#93;</div></p>
<h2>Meiosis I</h2>
<p>In meiosis I, <strong>homologous chromosomes</strong> are separated into two cells such that there is one chromosome (consisting of two chromatids) per chromosome pair in each daughter cell, i.e. two chromosomes total.</p>
<h3>Prophase I</h3>
<p>Prior to prophase, chromosomes replicate to form <strong>sister chromatids</strong>. There are initially four chromatids (c) and two chromosomes (n) for each of the 23 chromosome pairs (4c, 2n). The nuclear envelope disintegrates and the chromosomes begin to condense. <strong>Spindle fibres</strong> appear which are important for successful division of the chromosomes.</p>
<p>To further increase <strong>genetic diversity</strong>, homologous chromosomes exchange small parts of themselves, such that one chromosome contains both maternal and paternal DNA. This process is known as <strong>crossing over</strong>, and the points at which this occurs on a chromosome are referred to as <strong>chiasmata.</strong></p>
<h3>Prometaphase I</h3>
<p>Spindle fibres attach to the chromosomes at a points along the chromosomes called <strong>centromeres</strong>. While this is happening, the chromosomes continue to condense.</p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="caption">&#91;caption id="attachment_13754" align="aligncenter" width="300"&#93;<img class="size-medium wp-image-13754" src="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/ProphaseI-300x270.png" alt="" width="300" height="270" /> Figure 2 - Image of prometaphase I.&#91;/caption&#93;</div></p>
<h3>Metaphase I</h3>
<p>Maternal and paternal versions of the same chromosome <strong>(homologous chromosomes)</strong> align along the equator of the cell.<strong> </strong>A process called <strong>independent</strong><strong> assortment</strong> occurs – this is when maternal and paternal chromosomes line up randomly align themselves on either side of the equator. This is turn determines to which gamete chromosomes are allocated to, which leads to genetic diversity among offspring.</p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="caption">&#91;caption id="attachment_13755" align="aligncenter" width="268"&#93;<img class="size-medium wp-image-13755" src="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/MetaphaseI-268x300.png" alt="" width="268" height="300" /> Figure 3 - Image of Metaphase I&#91;/caption&#93;</div></p>
<h3>Anaphase I</h3>
<p>Here, each of the homologous chromosomes get pulled towards opposite poles of the cell as the spindle fibres retract. This equally divides the DNA between the two cells which will be formed.</p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="caption">&#91;caption id="attachment_13756" align="aligncenter" width="271"&#93;<img class="size-medium wp-image-13756" src="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Anaphase-I-271x300.png" alt="" width="271" height="300" /> Figure 4 - Image of Anaphase I.&#91;/caption&#93;</div></p>
<h3>Telophase I and Cytokinesis I</h3>
<p>During telophase I, the nuclear envelope reforms and spindle fibres disappear. In cytokinesis I, the cytoplasm and cell divides resulting in two cells that are technically haploid - there is one chromosome and two chromatids for each chromosome (2c, n).</p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="caption">&#91;caption id="attachment_13758" align="aligncenter" width="260"&#93;<img class="size-medium wp-image-13758" src="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Cytokinesis-I-and-Telophase-I-260x300.png" alt="" width="260" height="300" /> Figure 5 - Image of Telophase I and Cytokinesis I&#91;/caption&#93;</div></p>
<h2>Meiosis II</h2>
<h3>Prophase II and Prometaphase II</h3>
<p>These stages are identical to their counterparts in meiosis I.</p>
<h3>Metaphase II</h3>
<p>In metaphase II, chromosomes line up in single file along the equator of the cell. This is in contrast to metaphase I, where chromosomes line up in homologous pairs.</p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="caption">&#91;caption id="attachment_13759" align="aligncenter" width="300"&#93;<img class="size-medium wp-image-13759" src="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Metaphase-II-300x256.png" alt="" width="300" height="256" /> Figure 6 - Image of metaphase II&#91;/caption&#93;</div></p>
<h3>Anaphase II</h3>
<p>Next, <strong>sister chromatids</strong> are pulled to opposite poles of the equator.</p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="caption">&#91;caption id="attachment_13760" align="aligncenter" width="300"&#93;<img class="size-medium wp-image-13760" src="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/AnaphaseII-300x256.png" alt="" width="300" height="256" /> Figure 7 - Image of Anaphase II.&#91;/caption&#93;</div></p>
<h3>Telophase II</h3>
<p>This stage is the same as telophase I.</p>
<h3>Cytokinesis II</h3>
<p>Again, the cytoplasm and cell divides producing 2 non-identical haploid daughter cells. As this is happening in both cells produced by meiosis I, the net product is <strong>4 non-identical haploid daughter cells</strong>, each containing one chromosome consisting of one chromatid (1c, 1n). These are fully formed gametes.</p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="caption">&#91;caption id="attachment_13762" align="aligncenter" width="289"&#93;<img class="size-full wp-image-13762" src="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Telophase-II-and-Cytokinesis-II.png" alt="" width="289" height="263" /> Figure 8 - Image of Telophase II and Cytokinesis II&#91;/caption&#93;</div></p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="start-clinical">&#91;start-clinical&#93;</div></p>
<h2>Clinical Relevance - Chromosomal abnormalities</h2>
<p>Chromosome abnormalities occur in approximately 0.6% of live births, however are common in pregnancy losses. Errors in the process of meiosis can lead to abnormalities in either chromosome number and structure.</p>
<p><strong>Abnormalities in chromosome number</strong> include <strong>aneuploidy</strong>, where there is loss or gain of a whole chromosome. This is often due to <strong>nondisjunction</strong> where there is failed separation of chromosomes during anaphase, so either whole chromosomes (error occurring in meiosis I) or chromatids (error occurring in meiosis II) move to the same pole of the cell. This leaves one gamete short of some genetic information, and the other with additional genetic information.</p>
<ul>
<li>Monosomy - one copy of a chromosome, e.g. Turner syndrome (45, X)</li>
<li>Trisomy - three copies of a chromosome, e.g. Edward's syndrome (trisomy 18)</li></ul>
<p><strong>Abnormalities in chromosome structure </strong>are often due to translocations, where there is exchange of material between two chromosomes, resulting in an abnormal rearrangement. If there is no gain or loss of genetic material, this is a balanced translocation, however if the exchange of chromosomal material results in extra or missing genes in a daughter cell, it is known as unbalanced and can have clinical effects.</p>
<p>There are two types of chromosomal translocation. <strong>Reciprocal translocations</strong> take place when the chromosomes break within the arms of the chromosome and Robertsonian<strong> translocations</strong> take place when whole chromosomes join end to end.</p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="caption">&#91;caption id="attachment_19311" align="aligncenter" width="1024"&#93;<img class="size-full wp-image-19311" src="https://teachmephysiology.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/translocation.jpg" alt="" width="1024" height="408" /> Figure 9 - Reciprocal and Robertsonian translocation&#91;/caption&#93;</div></p>
<p><strong>Anaphase lag</strong> can occur where chromosomes are left behind due to defects in the spindle fibres or attachment to chromosomes. This differs from non-disjunction as neither cell receives the chromosome/chromatid, leaving both daughter cells short of genetic information</p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="end-clinical">&#91;end-clinical&#93;</div><strong><br />
</strong></p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="start-clinical">&#91;start-clinical&#93;</div></p>
<h2>Clinical Relevance - Down's syndrome</h2>
<p>Down's syndrome occurs when there is an extra copy of chromosome 21 (trisomy 21). Typical signs and symptoms include muscular hypotonia, a single palmar crease, protruding tongue, congenital heart malformations and distinguishable facies.</p>
<p>95% of cases occur when there are three separate copies of chromosome 21 due to nondisjunction. 4% of cases are secondary to Robertsonian translocation, where a chromosome acquires a large chunk of chromosome 21, effectively leading to three copies. The remaining 1% of cases are due to <strong>post-zygotic mosaicism</strong>, where non-disjunction occurs in early embryonic development, meaning some cells have 46 chromosomes as usual, while others have trisomy 21. This generally leads to milder symptoms due to the presence of normal cells.</p>
<p><div class="shortcode" data-name="end-clinical">&#91;end-clinical&#93;</div></p>
